01 — Overview
Context & Problem

Putumayo built its identity on making the world feel smaller — bright, illustrated CDs of world music, stacked next to your coffee at Starbucks. It worked. For a while. Then streaming arrived and so did the cultural depth that made Putumayo worth caring about.

But the problem runs deeper. Putumayo's curation has always been shaped by a Western editorial lens — Asia reduced to Middle Eastern sounds, Africa collapsed into a single category despite hundreds of distinct cultures.

Insight 01
Cultural Bias in Curation
Asia is largely absent beyond Middle Eastern music. Africa's vast diversity is collapsed into a single category. Users experience music without cultural context.
Insight 02
Passive Consumption — No Depth
No mechanism for users to understand why a track belongs to a culture. Low retention, low emotional connection, no advocacy behaviour.
Insight 03
Identity & Heritage Matter
Users from underrepresented cultures feel unseen or misrepresented. Cultural music is deeply tied to family, identity, and memory.

Hard to find trustworthy experiences

CJM Key Finding

The Customer Journey Map revealed a critical drop-off: Janet starts curious, but becomes emotionally disengaged during Use and Retention stages. High initial curiosity → rapid emotional disengagement → no advocacy.

04 — Hypothesis
Strategic Direction
If we...
Shift Putumayo from a centralised curatorial authority to a participatory model — where regional artists, local record labels, and audiences co-create the experience —
Then users will develop a deeper cultural connection and perceive the brand as trustworthy and authentic.
Principle 01
Transparent
Show why music is categorised the way it is. Transparent curation notes and cultural context at every touchpoint.
Principle 02
Trustworthy
Co-curate with regional partners, not just Western editors. Arnstein's Ladder — toward Citizen Control in playlisting.
Principle 03
Participatory
Users contribute to the next playlist, not just consume. From passive listener to active co-creator of cultural value.
05 — Solution
WOMAD Pop-up 2026
3-Zone Experience

A three-zone cultural journey at WOMAD Festival — moving visitors from passive absorption to active co-creation, leaving with genuine cultural understanding and a contribution to Putumayo's next playlist.

Zone 1
Music Discovery
Six continent-based jukeboxes co-curated with regional record labels. Transparent curation notes and cultural video content. Visitors select postcards with QR codes — their choice becomes soft data for the next Putumayo playlist.
→ Increased perceived authenticity and brand trust
Educational
Zone 2
Motion Communication
Motion-capture visuals respond to dance in real time — abstract shapes for full inclusivity. Sound becomes shared sensory interaction at the emotional peak of the experience. Culture felt through the body, not just the ear.
→ Peak emotional engagement, embodied cultural participation
Escapist
Zone 3
Creative Workshop
Participants colour illustrated postcards and "send" them via a postbox scanner. Their preference data informs future playlist curation. Staff explain data usage clearly — consent-based participation throughout.
→ User becomes co-creator, reciprocal artist-audience relationship

06 — Personal Extension
AI Curating Service Concept

The pop-up generates real behavioural data — which tracks people gravitated toward, which postcards they chose, how they moved in Zone 2. This is seed data for a bias-aware AI curation engine.

Feature 01
Cultural Context Engine
Tags each track by instrument, rhythm origin, and cultural tradition — not just geography. Replacing broad generalisations like "African music".
Feature 02
Bias Detection Layer
Flags when a regional category is under-represented or over-generalised. Structurally fairer curation addressing Asia/Africa blind spots.
Feature 03
Explainable Curation
Every recommendation includes why it was suggested and what culture it comes from. Music you feel, culture you understand.
Product Vision
"Music you feel. Culture you understand."

08 — Reflection
What I Learned
83
Academic score
out of 100
"Designing for cultural experiences isn't just about aesthetics — it's about power."

Who gets to decide what "world music" sounds like? This project pushed me to apply UX frameworks not just to improve usability, but to challenge the ethical structure of a product itself.

The AI product concept isn't just academic. I want to build systems that make algorithmic curation more transparent and culturally accountable.

◈
Cultural bias is invisible to those it doesn't affect
Surface it through research, not assumption. The 1:1 interviews were the most important part.
◎
Co-creation is most powerful when the output is real
Participants shaped an actual future playlist — not a fictional exercise. That made engagement genuine.
◇
Ethical design isn't a constraint — it was the experience
Moving toward Citizen Control became the design brief itself, not just a consideration.
○
Physical data seeds AI in ways digital data can't
Postcard selections and motion data carry embodied preference signals that passive streaming misses.
